Oncogenes

Description

All multicellular organisms including humans, plants and everything inbetween, have the need of generating new cells to handle growth, wound healing, and many other things.

Cell Division

The cells in the body multiply by cell division, i.e. it creates two identical copies itself. This process is also called the cell cycle.

Most of the time, the cells are in a state called G0 where no cell division is occuring. When the need of new cells arise, the cell will enter the phase G1 and start growing. The next step is to copy the DNA in the cell in a process called DNA replication. The cell is considered to be in the S (synthesis) phase while the replication occurs. After the S phase, the cell continues to grow in the G2 phase, until it finally divides into two new cells in a process called mitosis (the M phase).

There are a lot of control mechanisms in place during the cell cycle in order to avoid errors and unneccessary cell division. These mechanisms consist of proteins that are produced by the organism.

Control Mechanisms

While the control mechanisms work very well, misstakes may still happen. When the DNA is replicated in the S phase, small errors (mutations) may show up in the new DNA copy. In most cases these small variations are completely harmless, but sometimes they may occur in important parts, e.g. in genes (the parts that encode proteins).

If a mutation occurs in one of the genes encoding the proteins for the cell cycle control mechanisms, they might start to malfunction. The problem is probably minor at first, but may over time cause problems in other parts of the control system.

Oncogenes

An oncogene is a gene that has the potential to cause cancer. In many cases, these oncogenes are encoding proteins that are involved in the control mechanisms of the cell cycle. Other functions of oncogenes include the encoding of proteins that control the motility of the cells (their ability to move) or the formation of blood vessels.

Many common cancer-causing mutations in oncogenes lead to over- (too much protein is formed) or underexpression (too little protein is formed). If too much of a protein that is responsible for starting phase G1 is formed, or a protein that is supposed to prevent this is not formed at all, the cell might start the cell cycle when it is not supposed to. A tumor is formed.

Assignment

In order to get the password, you will need to perform some analysis of the sequences of a couple of oncogenes. Some of the genes are mutated, either in a critical or non-critical way.

In order to evaluate each gene, you will have to:

  1. Obtain the canonincal (native) sequence for the gene from UniProt
  2. Align the potentially mutated sequence to the canonical (native) sequence for the gene
  3. Identify the mutation(s)
  4. List the mutations in the standard form, e.g. A15G. This means that the canonical sequence has an alanine in position 15 (the first residue is position 1), which in the mutated sequence is a glycine. Always base the numbering on the canonical sequence.

For alignments, it is recommended to use one of the following:

  • The built-in functionality of UniProt
  • Needle
  • Kalign

The final password that should be entered into coordmaker are the mutations in sequential order. E.g. if the first protein has the mutations A3G and C2A, and the second protein has the mutations P1A and C2D, then the final password is C2AA3GP1AC2D
